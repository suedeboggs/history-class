MMXXVI
A Curated World History Curriculum

The Long Arc

History repeats itself — first as tragedy, then as farce, then as America.
This course is built around one idea: no civilization exists in isolation. From Babylon to Boston, from the Nile to Nagasaki, the same patterns of rise, overreach, revolution, and reinvention play out across centuries. Your task is to learn to see them.
I
The Course at a Glance

How to Use This Curriculum

This isn't a textbook — it's a conversation across time. Each unit pairs civilizations you know with ones you're discovering, and traces the invisible wires connecting them to the United States and to each other.

The Central Thesis

Every culture you love shares the same drama: a people build something extraordinary, overreach it, watch it fracture, and bequeath the wreckage to the next civilization, who calls it their founding myth. The American founders knew their Rome. Napoleon knew his Caesar. Jefferson knew his Thucydides. History isn't cyclical by coincidence — it's cyclical because humans don't change.

II
Unit One

Ancient Worlds: Babylon, Egypt & Rome

The civilizations you know best — Rome and Egypt — are inseparable from ones you'll want to know better. Babylon gave the world its first law code, its first epic literature, its first bureaucracy. Egypt gave the world monumental time. Rome synthesized everything before it and handed it to the West.

3500 – 500 BCE
Mesopotamia & Babylon — The World's First Empire Builders
Babylon wasn't just a city — it was an idea: that a king could unite disparate peoples under one law, one language, one god. Hammurabi's Code (1754 BCE) is the first written legal system. The Epic of Gilgamesh predates Homer by a thousand years and asks the same question American literature still asks: what is a man's legacy?
Why it connects to you The US Constitution's structure of codified law — enforceable, written, supreme — descends directly from this tradition. The concept of a republic governed by law rather than a monarch's whim starts here, not in Rome.

3100 – 30 BCE
Ancient Egypt — Power, Monument & Eternity
Egypt invented the idea that a state could project permanence — through architecture, art, and religion. The pharaohs weren't kings; they were living gods. Understanding Egypt means understanding how societies use art and spectacle to legitimize power — a lesson that Napoleon, Louis XIV, and the US Capitol's architects all absorbed directly.
Why it connects to you Napoleon's Egyptian campaign (1798) directly shaped French art, politics, and Europe's obsession with antiquity. The Washington Monument is an obelisk — deliberate Egyptian symbolism. The Founding Fathers were infatuated with Egypt as a symbol of enduring civilization.

753 BCE – 476 CE
Rome — The Blueprint for Everything That Followed
Rome is not just a civilization — it's the operating system the Western world runs on. The Republic, the Senate, the concept of citizenship, the rule of law, the standing army, the road network, the aqueduct. And then the fall — which every subsequent civilization has used as a warning and a mirror. The American Founders were obsessed with Rome. They called themselves Publius, Cato, and Brutus.
Why it connects to you The US Senate is named after Rome's. Roman fasces appear on the US Mercury dime and the Lincoln Memorial. The Founders debated Caesar and Cicero in their letters the way we debate Twitter. Baseball's stadiums — Fenway's Roman brick arch, the modern coliseum shape — are Roman inheritances.

III
Unit Two

The Age of Revolutions: America, France & Haiti

Within 15 years, three revolutions redrew the map of the Western world — and each gave a different answer to the question: what do we do with liberty once we have it? Studying them together is the key to understanding all three.

The Triangle You Need to Know

The American Revolution inspired the French. The French Revolution inspired the Haitian. The Haitian Revolution terrified the American south and accelerated the Louisiana Purchase. All three were happening simultaneously and watching each other. Jefferson wrote his Declaration in Paris. He watched the French Revolution from the American embassy and didn't quite know what to make of it.

1776 – 1800
The American Revolution — A Roman Experiment
The founders didn't invent their ideas — they assembled them from Locke, Montesquieu, Roman history, and English common law. What made the American founding remarkable wasn't the theory but the execution: that it produced a stable republic, not a Caesar. But it was a close-run thing, and the slavery compromise was the crack in the foundation that would split the structure 80 years later.
The Cyclical Connection Roman senators worried about demagogues with armies. The Founders built the Electoral College and the Senate partly to stop an American Caesar. They read Polybius on how republics fall. The debate about whether republics inevitably become empires is still live.

1789 – 1799
The French Revolution — Liberty's Darker Twin
The French Revolution began inspired by America and ended with a military dictator. Why? Because France lacked what America had: a civic culture already practiced in self-governance, a federal structure, and — crucially — an ocean between them and their enemies. The Revolution ate its own children because the stakes of failure were existential. This is the most important story in modern history for understanding how democracies fail.
The Cyclical Connection The Terror, the Thermidorian reaction, the Directory, Napoleon — each phase has a pattern. Radical revolution produces counter-revolution. Ideological purity produces purges. Purges produce exhaustion. Exhaustion produces a strongman. Watch this sequence repeat in Russia 1917, China 1949, Iran 1979. The American founding was the exception, not the rule.

1791 – 1804
The Haitian Revolution — The One They Don't Teach
The only successful slave revolt in history. Haiti (then Saint-Domingue) was France's richest colony, producing 40% of Europe's sugar. Enslaved people, inspired by the Declaration of the Rights of Man, rose up, defeated Napoleon's army, and founded the world's first Black republic in 1804. The US refused to recognize Haiti for 58 years. Jefferson, who wrote "all men are created equal," embargoed Haiti because he was terrified of what it meant.
Why it matters to US history Napoleon's failure in Haiti directly caused him to sell Louisiana to the US in 1803. Without Haiti's revolution, there is no Louisiana Purchase, and US westward expansion looks completely different. The revolution that American history barely mentions shaped American geography entirely.

Unit Three

Empire & Collapse: Napoleon, Louis XIV & the Arc of Power

What do you do after a revolution? Every society that tears down its old order faces the same crisis: a vacuum of authority that something — or someone — will fill. Napoleon is the answer France gave. Louis XIV is the answer that made Napoleon necessary. Together they frame the question every superpower eventually faces: how much is too much?

1643 – 1715
Louis XIV & Versailles — Power Made Visible
The Sun King invented the modern state as spectacle. Versailles was a political weapon — it gathered the nobility in one place, bankrupted them with the cost of court life, and kept them dependent on royal favor. His famous line "L'état, c'est moi" (I am the state) describes a system that made the French state utterly dependent on the king's personal genius. When a lesser man inherited it, the whole system collapsed — producing the conditions for the Revolution.
The Cyclical Connection Louis's system is the story of institutional brittleness: a structure built for one exceptional person cannot survive ordinary succession. This pattern recurs with Napoleon, with Rome's emperors after Augustus, with the Soviet Union after Stalin.

1799 – 1815
Napoleon — The Revolution's Heir and Its Betrayal
Napoleon is one of history's great Rorschach tests. Liberal reformer or military dictator? Heir of the Revolution or its gravedigger? Both are true. He spread the Napoleonic Code (based on revolutionary principles) across Europe while crowning himself Emperor. His rise and fall is the template for modern authoritarianism — and for the limits of military genius in politics. He won every battle and lost every war.
Why it connects to you Napoleon directly shaped the US: the Louisiana Purchase; the Continental System that drove the War of 1812; the inspiration for how 19th-century American presidents imagined executive power. His legal code is still the basis for Louisiana state law today.

Unit Four — New Territory

Japan & Latin America: Worlds Colliding

These are the two regions you said fascinate you but feel unknown. They share a remarkable pattern: both experienced catastrophic contact with outside powers that forced total transformation — and both developed fierce national identities in response. Start here for the biggest expansion of your historical imagination.

The Pattern to Watch

The Aztec and Inca empires were not Stone Age societies crushed by advanced Europeans. They were sophisticated civilizations with millions of people, complex politics, writing, astronomy, and agriculture. What defeated them was primarily disease — and the political fragmentation that European disease and weaponry accelerated. Japan faced the same Western pressure centuries later, but had the isolation and unity to resist and adapt. The comparison is illuminating.

1200 – 1600 CE
The Aztec & Inca Empires — America's Ancient Worlds
Tenochtitlan (today's Mexico City) in 1500 was larger than any city in Europe. The Inca Empire stretched 4,000 km along the Andes and had no writing system yet administered millions of people through an extraordinary quipu (knotted string) record system. These weren't primitive cultures — they were peer civilizations to contemporary European states. The Spanish conquest is not a story of superiority; it's a story of disease, diplomacy, and catastrophic luck.
Why it connects to US history The colonization of Latin America set the template for US westward expansion. The idea that indigenous peoples were savage and land was empty — terra nullius — was first developed to justify Spanish and Portuguese conquest, then adopted wholesale by Anglo settlers. Understanding Cortés explains Andrew Jackson.

1600 – 1900
Japan: From Isolation to Empire — The Fastest Transformation in History
For 250 years under the Tokugawa shogunate, Japan was deliberately sealed from the outside world. Foreigners were expelled; Japanese were forbidden to leave; a rigid feudal hierarchy froze society in place. Then in 1853, US Commodore Matthew Perry arrived with steam-powered warships and demanded trade access — and Japan's world shattered. What followed — the Meiji Restoration — was perhaps the most remarkable national transformation ever achieved: within 40 years, Japan went from feudal samurai culture to an industrialized imperial power that defeated Russia.
Why it connects to US history The US literally opened Japan — Perry's "Black Ships" are one of the most consequential moments in American foreign policy. The chain runs directly: Perry → Meiji industrialization → Japanese imperial expansion → Pearl Harbor → Hiroshima → Cold War Pacific strategy. This is a thread you can follow from 1853 to today.

Unit Five

The Cold War: Ideology as the New Religion

The Cold War was not primarily a military conflict — it was a war of ideas, myths, and competing visions of human organization. In this it resembled the wars of religion that tore Europe apart in the 16th and 17th centuries. Understanding it requires understanding both sides' foundational stories — and the dozens of countries caught between them.

The Baseball Connection

Baseball becomes the Cold War's cultural battlefield in ways that are deeply revealing. Jackie Robinson's integration (1947) was deployed by the US State Department as propaganda against Soviet claims of American racism. The 1955 Boys of Summer Dodgers were a multicultural team in a segregated city — a microcosm of American contradiction. Cuban baseball becomes a Cold War flashpoint after 1959. The 1972 US-USSR hockey game at the World Championships was called "the war the Cold War almost became."

1945 – 1991
The Cold War — America at the Height of Its Power
The Cold War is where American history becomes world history. Every conflict from Korea to Vietnam to Afghanistan to the Middle East has roots in the post-WWII competition between US and Soviet spheres of influence. But the most important Cold War stories are often the ones that happened in the margins — in Guatemala, Chile, Iran, and the Congo — where American intervention toppled governments, installed dictators, and created the anti-American sentiment that still shapes global politics.
The Cyclical Connection Compare the Cold War to Rome's relationship with its client states, or Britain's 19th-century empire. Every great power develops a "sphere of influence" rationale that justifies intervention in weaker nations' affairs. The Monroe Doctrine (1823) is the template; the Cold War is its fullest expression.

VII
Unit Six — The Through-Line

The Cycles of History

Once you've moved through the units above, you'll start seeing patterns that cut across centuries and continents. This final unit names them — and asks where the United States sits in these cycles right now.

The Succession Problem

Every exceptional leader creates a system that cannot survive their departure. Augustus → Roman civil wars. Louis XIV → Revolution. Napoleon → Restoration. Stalin → Soviet stagnation.

Ask: How do the US's institutional guardrails compare to Rome's?

Revolution Devours Its Children

The idealists who start revolutions are almost never the people who end up running the resulting state. Robespierre was guillotined. Trotsky was exiled and murdered. The Cuban revolution's poets were imprisoned.

Ask: Who got purged in the American founding? (Aaron Burr, the Federalists)

Overreach & Retrenchment

Every empire at its peak makes the same mistake: it projects power beyond its logistical or political capacity to sustain. Athens in Sicily. Napoleon in Russia. America in Vietnam and Iraq.

Ask: Where is the US in this cycle today?

Disease as History

The Antonine Plague weakened Rome. The Black Death reshuffled European society. COVID-19 in the Americas killed entire civilizations before Europeans arrived. Epidemics reshape political power faster than any army.

Ask: What political changes followed each pandemic?

The Barbarian Paradox

Every dominant civilization defines "barbarian" to mean "not us" — then discovers the barbarians have virtues it has lost. Rome absorbed its Germanic conquerors. The Mongols absorbed Chinese culture. America made its greatest music from its most oppressed people.

Ask: Who are today's "barbarians"? And what do they have that we've lost?

Art as Evidence

Every civilization leaves art that tells you exactly who it thought it was. Egyptian art is timeless and divine. Roman art is realistic and triumphal. American art (from Whitman to jazz to baseball) is democratic and anxious about its own greatness.

Ask: What does this era's art say about us?

Your Reading Path

Where to Start: A Sequenced On-Ramp

The full curriculum is deep — so here's a sequenced path of twelve books and four shows that build on each other and give you the maximum foundation in the minimum time.

1

Start: The Lessons of History — Will & Ariel Durant

100 pages that survey all of human history's recurring patterns. Read this first and every other book becomes richer. It's the map before the territory.

2

Ancient: SPQR — Mary Beard

The best modern Roman history. Read alongside Mike Duncan's "History of Rome" podcast. This is the foundation for understanding the American founding.

3

Watch: HBO's Rome (2005–2007)

Two seasons. Watch it while reading Beard. The visual grammar of Roman politics will stick with you.

4

Revolution: Citizens — Simon Schama

The French Revolution in full. Long but gripping. Pairs with the Versailles series on Netflix.

5

Revolution: The Black Jacobins — C.L.R. James

Read this immediately after Schama. It will reframe everything you just read about the French Revolution from a perspective you haven't encountered before.

6

Napoleon: Napoleon: A Life — Andrew Roberts

Then watch Waterloo (1970). By this point you'll have a complete picture of the Revolutionary era across three continents.

7

Americas: 1491 — Charles Mann

This book will completely reframe your understanding of pre-Columbian America. Follow it with Bernal Díaz's Conquest of New Spain for the Spanish side of the story.

8

Watch: Shogun (FX, 2024)

Ten episodes of television that will give you a complete immersion in Tokugawa Japan — its politics, aesthetics, and the collision with the West. One of the best historical dramas ever made.

9

Japan: Japan: A History — R.H.P. Mason

Read this after Shogun to give the full arc from Tokugawa isolation through the Meiji transformation to WWII.

10

Cold War: The Cold War: A World History — Odd Arne Westad

The definitive single-volume account that covers every continent, not just US-Soviet relations. Then watch The Americans (FX) — all six seasons.

11

America: Are We Rome? — Cullen Murphy

By now you have enough context to read this comparative study and evaluate it critically. You'll find yourself agreeing and disagreeing with specific arguments — which means you're thinking historically.

12

Egypt & Babylon: The Rise and Fall of Ancient Egypt — Toby Wilkinson

Save this for last. After reading about Rome, the French Revolution, and the Cold War, ancient Egypt will feel surprisingly familiar — the same arguments about succession, legitimacy, and imperial overreach, just 4,000 years earlier.

The Question to Carry

As you read, always ask: What did they think would last forever? The Pharaohs built in stone. Rome built in law. The Soviet Union built in ideology. America built in constitutional institutions and economic hegemony. Every civilization mistakes its current arrangements for permanent ones. The study of history is, in part, the study of that mistake — and perhaps the only way to make it a little less catastrophic the next time.
